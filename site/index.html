The problem, plainly

You're accountable for work you don't do yourself. The promises that sink you live in side threads and meeting notes — "I should have something by the 30th." Search can't find a date you don't know exists. A list can't hold a promise nobody wrote down. And every tool that could is homework somebody has to do forever.

What Threshold does

How it works.

1

Forward it. That's the whole workflow.

Email, note, screenshot, recording — Threshold files every promise it finds, in the promiser's exact words, and replies with a receipt. If it finds nothing, the receipt says so.

2

See two weeks ahead.

Today opens on everything coming due — who owns it, what's drifting, what's gone quiet. Amber before it's late, not after, with a short no-blame heads-up staged one tap away. Nothing sends itself.

3

Start the day with a plan. End it with less on it.

Three times a day, your companion walks the list with you. Items it can take, it takes — it drafts, you comment, it revises. Your work, off your plate, still your name on it.

No homework

You've tried the boards and the color-coded lists — every one of them asks somebody to keep it current, forever. That's the job that never gets done. Threshold doesn't ask: the filing, the organizing, and the keeping-current happen on their own, and when it isn't sure, it asks you one small question instead of handing you a system to manage.

Trust, stated plainly

Threshold only sees what you forward or connect. Every claim it makes carries a receipt — the exact words, the source document, the date. When it can't see enough to judge, it says so and asks instead of guessing. Every plan is correctable in place — mark a step done, move the date, undo any of it. And you choose where the reading happens: our models, or keys you control.
